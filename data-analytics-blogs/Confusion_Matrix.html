·      What is a confusion matrix?

 

To understand what a confusion matrix is we must first understand the following 6 terms:

-       True Positive

-       True Negative

-       False Positive

-       False Negative

-       Precision

-       Recall

-       Accuracy

 

Believe it or not it took me some time to wrap my head around on what the terms True Positive, True Negative, False Positive, and False Negative actually mean. I am here to assure people like me who struggled that its very simple to understand. I understood it this way:

o   Let’s take False Positive as an example. Equate (False Positive) to (M N). Always, remember N is what the home test kit determined. Example: My home test kit told me that my test result for covid-19 is N. M is what the actual result is, but the way we find the actual results is by using the sentence “What is M of N?” this tells us the answer.

§  Stay with me. Now let me use (False Positive) in a real-world example:

I tested myself with one of those rapid home testing kits to see if I have covid-19 or not. Test results says I am Positive.

I go to my doctor and she tests me again for covid-19 but with a PCR test which has a good track record for being accurate.

A few hours later my doctor calls me and says “Hi, your home kit test result is False Positive. Bye!”

She hangs up and now I am wondering what she meant. I then remember that the way to decipher this is by asking “What is M of N?”, so I ask, “What is False of Positive?” and naturally the answer is “Negative”. I am elated and thankful now that I have not contracted the virus.

 

Therefore, the following are what these terms mean:

 

-       True Positive: I really have covid-19

§  Home test kit said I am covid positive.

§  After PCR test from the doctor, she calls and tells me ‘Hi, your home test kit result is True Positive, Bye!”

§  “What is M of N?”, so I ask, “What is True of Positive?” and naturally the answer is “Positive”.

 

-       True Negative: I don’t have covid-19

§  Home test kit said I am covid nagative.

§  After PCR test from the doctor, she calls and tells me ‘Hi, your home test kit result is True Negative, Bye!”

§  “What is M of N?”, so I ask, “What is True of Negative?” and naturally the answer is “Negative”.

 

-       False Positive: I don’t have covid-19

§  Home test kit said I am covid positive.

§  After PCR test from the doctor, she calls and tells me ‘Hi, your home test kit result is False Positive, Bye!”

§  “What is M of N?”, so I ask, “What is False of Positive?” and naturally the answer is “Negative”.

 

-       False Negative: I really have covid-19

§  Home test kit said I am covid negative.

§  After PCR test from the doctor, she calls and tells me ‘Hi, your home test kit result is False Negative, Bye!”

§  “What is M of N?”, so I ask, “What is False of Negative?” and naturally the answer is “Positive”.

 

 

-       Now use the following example:

 

§  A teacher decided to give all of the class of 100 student a rapid covid-19 test kit, and found the following results:

·      55 students are positive

·      45 students are negative

o   The 55 students decide to go and get a PCR test and find the following results:

§  25 students are positive –(a)

§  30 students are negative –(b)

o   The 45 students decide to go and get a PCR test and find the following results:

§  10 students are positive –(c)

§  35 students are negative –(d)

 

Therefore, our confusion matrix would have:

            TP:       25        (25 students out of 55 students are truly positive)

            TN:      30        (45 students are negative out of which only 35 students are really negative)

            FP:       30        (55 students were predicted positive but only 25-(a) students in there are positive. Hence, |55-(25)| = 30)

            FN:       10        (45 students are negative out of which only 10 students are actually positive)

 

            Actual Positives: 35

            Actual Negatives: 65

 

            Predicted Positives: 55

            Predicted Negatives: 45

-       Recall (True positive rate or Sensitivity): The formula is [True_Positive]/[Actual Positives]. How often does the model accurately predict actual positive cases?

 

·      Recall = 25/(35) = 0.71

·      Example: On Amazon search for a product how often is my model able to find the correct results.

 

-       Precision: The formula is [True_Positive]/[Predicted Positives]. Whenever the model predicts positive cases how often is it right?

 

·      Recall = 25/(55) = 0.45

·      Example: On Amazon search for a product, out of the products displayed in the search how many are actually what the customer is looking for?

 

-       Accuracy: The formula is [True_Positive + True_Negative]/[Total]. This tells us how often our classifier is correct:

 

·      Accuracy = 60/100 = 0.6

 

-       Misclassification rate = 1 – accuracy

 

·      Misclassification rate = 1 – 0.71 = 0.29

 

-       Specificity: How often does the model rightly predict the real negative cases. Formula = [TN]/[Actual Negatives]

 

·      Specificity = 35/65 = 0.53

 

-       F-score: If I go on Amazon.com and search for a product and out of the 20 listed products only 3 matches, then that would mean the recall is really low. So, we need to make sure our classifier model is smart to give us the right results. Just by measuring precision and recall we won’t be able to get there or compare which one of the two models is good. F1 score comes into play to measure some of precision and some of recall. F1 score can be measure by = (2*Precision*Recall)/(Precision + Recall).

 

 

-       Type 1 & Type 2 error:

Suppose I get a call from an unknow phone number, the caller says “You just won the lottery! Quick, give me your bank details so I could transfer the money.”

We all know that a call like this would most likely be a call from a scammer.

Hence, our null hypothesis is -> H0: This call is a scam.

Our alternative hypothesis is -> This call is actually genuine.

 

If! This call is really a scam and I still give the caller my bank details then I would have made the Type 1 error (I just got my identity stolen), because I rejected the null hypothesis (which is true in this case) in that moment and decided the alternative hypothesis is true.

 

If! This call is actually genuine, and I still hang-up on the caller thinking this is a scam then I would have made the Type 2 error (I just lost my opportunity to claim the lottery), because I rejected the alternative hypothesis (which is true in this case) and accepted the null hypothesis.
