·      What is Bias-Variance tradeoff?

 

Let’s understand in plain English what the words Bias and Variance mean.

 

-       Bias:         

 

Bias is the distance between the predicted values and the actual values.

 

 

                        Credit: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Residuals_for_Linear_Regression_Fit.png

 

o   Low Bias means the distance between the predicted values and the actual values (aka residuals) are low. We always want a result with low bias.

 

o   High Bias means the distance between the predicted values and the actual values (aka residuals) are high.

 

-       Variance:

 

Great way to understand variance is just to look at how a model performs on a training data. Variance tells us how well the model fits on a training data.

 

o   High variance happens when a model goes out of its way to adjust itself to each and every data point, this is called overfitting. (We would also notice the bias is very low here as well)

 

Credit: https://www.nimblecoding.com/static/blog/over-under-fitting/

 

o    Low variance happens when the model performs loosely on the training data and by missing a lot of data points, this is called underfitting. (We would also notice the bias is very high here as well).

 

Credit: https://community.alteryx.com/t5/Data-Science/Bias-Versus-Variance/ba-p/351862

 

 

When a given data has few attributes to work with or lose hyperparameters it can make a model that underfits, this happens with low model complexity.

 

When a given data has a lot of attributes to work with or very tight hyperparameters it can make a model that overfits, this happens with high model complexity.

 

Therefore, when the model complexity is low, we notice signs of underfitting with:

§  High bias

§  Low variance

 

Therefore, when the model complexity is high, we notice signs of overfitting (on training data) with:

§  Low bias

§  High variance

 

 

Since the model is constructed through training data, we would notice the rate of error (rate of bias) goes down and variance goes up as model complexity increases. This is perfect for the training data but horrible for the test data (and the real-world data), as the overfit model made out of the training data would start predicting the test data, this would generate high rates of error (high bias and low variance).

 

Therefore, our aim should always be to come up with a model that has medium complexity so it can present us with low bias (predicts well) and has low variance (doesn’t overfit the training data).
